The Revolving Internet (2010)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Revolving Internet is a browser-based artwork created by Constant Dullaart in 2010. The piece presents the Google homepage rotating continuously through 360 degrees while remaining fully functional. Users can still type, search, and click while the entire interface spins.

Development and technical aspects

The work was created using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, employing an iframe to embed the live Google page and applying a continuous rotation transform. It quickly became a viral sensation, attracting approximately 1.5 million visits per year.

Due to subsequent changes in Google’s embedding policy, the original version is no longer functional. The artwork now survives through online archives such as Rhizome’s Net Art Anthology, illustrating the ephemerality of digital art and its dependence on corporate infrastructures.

Concept and interpretation

According to Rhizome, the project “makes the user aware that Google is not God.” The rotating interface subverts one of the most stable and trusted digital environments, creating both physical and cognitive discomfort. By disrupting habitual orientation, it exposes the extent to which interface design shapes perception and trust in digital systems.

Critical reception

The piece has been described as an example of “experiential critique,” revealing the psychological and behavioral influence of online interfaces. Designers and scholars have noted that even subtle alterations to digital environments can influence comfort, attention, and credibility.
